But here's something I want you to hear: the fact that you're still showing up, even when it feels like this, actually says something. It says you haven't given up on Him. And I don't think that's small. I think God sees that — the quiet faithfulness when nothing feels like it's working.

Scripture says, "The Lord is near to the brokenhearted and saves the crushed in spirit" (Psalm 34:18). That's not a promise that you'll always *feel* His presence. It's a promise that He's close *especially* when you feel furthest from Him.

I'm wondering — have you told Him any of this? Like, just laid it out honestly — "I don't know if You're noticing. I don't know if this matters"? Sometimes the most faithful prayer is the one that just names what's true, even if it doesn't sound very spiritual.

## 8. Doubt

_(guest_id: `scenario-e3158cd3`)_

**Opener:** Hi. I'm glad you're here — take your time.

What's been on your heart lately? Something on your mind, something about how your walk with God is going, or just something you've been carrying?

**User:** I've been having doubts. Not about God existing — more about whether Christianity is really THE way. I have friends who are Muslim and Buddhist and they're good people. It's hard to believe they're just… wrong.

**Companion:** That's a really honest question. And it's one a lot of people feel but don't say out loud, so I'm glad you did.

I hear two things in what you're saying. One is about your friends — people you care about, who are kind and sincere. The other is about truth — whether there's really one way, or whether that feels too narrow.

Can I say something I've noticed? The goodness you see in your friends is real. Christianity doesn't teach that non-Christians can't be good or loving — we actually believe the opposite. Every person is made in God's image, and that means His light shows up in people everywhere, whether they know Him by name or not. Your Muslim and Buddhist friends reflect something of God because they bear His image. That's not a contradiction — it's what we'd expect.

But here's the harder part. Christianity makes a specific claim that's different from saying "all roads lead to the same place." It says that humanity's deepest problem isn't just moral failure or ignorance — it's separation from God because of sin. And the solution isn't us finding our way back through effort or enlightenment. It's God coming to us in Jesus, dying in our place, and rising to make reconciliation possible. That's not one option among many. It's either true or it isn't.

Jesus Himself said, "I am the way, and the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me." That's not me being narrow — that's Him. And if He's right, then the question isn't whether good people exist in other religions. It's whether Jesus is who He said He is.

Does that distinction make sense? I'm not trying to shut down your question — I'm trying to help you see what the question is really about.

**Saved:**
- _struggle_: I've been doubting whether Christianity is the only way, especially when I see my Muslim and Buddhist friends being good people.

## 11. Look beneath the symptom (lust)

_(guest_id: `scenario-b39572fb`)_

**Opener:** Hi. I'm glad you're here — take your time.

What's been on your heart lately? Something on your mind, something about how your walk with God is going, or just something you've been carrying?

**User:** I've been struggling with lust again. It keeps happening on nights I'm home alone. I don't know what's wrong with me.

**Companion:** I'm really glad you told me. That takes courage — and it's the right place to start.

There's nothing wrong with you for struggling with this. You're human, and this is one of the most common battles people face. The fact that you're naming it instead of hiding it? That's already faithfulness.

I'm noticing something in what you said — it sounds like this shows up most when you're alone at night. That pattern is worth paying attention to. Lust often grows in loneliness, boredom, or exhaustion. It promises relief or connection, but leaves us emptier.

What does it feel like right before it happens? Like, what's going on in you — tired, restless, lonely, something else?

**User:** Yeah, honestly, most nights I feel pretty alone. I moved for work last year and haven't made real friends here yet.

**Companion:** That's really hard. Moving to a new place and not having people around — that kind of loneliness weighs on you in ways that are easy to underestimate.

I think what you just said is actually the deeper thing. The lust isn't the root — it's what shows up when the loneliness gets too heavy to sit with. It's like your heart is reaching for something to fill that ache, even if it's something that can't actually do it.

That doesn't make the lust okay, but it does mean the real work isn't just about saying no in the moment. It's about tending to the loneliness underneath. You can't white-knuckle your way out of this without also addressing what's driving it.

So two things I'm wondering:

First — what would it look like to actually pursue friendship here, even if it feels slow or awkward? Like, is there one person at work, or church if you're going, or even a neighbor, you could reach out to this week?

And second — on those nights when you're home alone and the restlessness starts, what if instead of fighting it in your own strength, you brought the loneliness itself to God? Not just "help me not sin," but "I'm lonely and I don't know what to do with it."

**Saved:**
- _struggle_: I'm struggling with lust, especially on nights when I'm home alone.
- _struggle_: I'm feeling pretty alone since I moved for work last year and haven't made real friends here yet.
